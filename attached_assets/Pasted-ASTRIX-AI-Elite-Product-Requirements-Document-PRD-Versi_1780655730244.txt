1. Product definition
ASTRIX AI is a product decision and accountability platform for B2B SaaS teams. It transforms scattered customer feedback into structured problems, ranks those problems as opportunities, records product decisions with evidence, and tracks whether shipped work actually solved the underlying issue.

The core value proposition is simple: product teams should be able to answer two questions with proof — what should be built next, and did it work after launch. A strong PRD should define the problem, users, scope, features, technical requirements, success metrics, and release criteria clearly enough that design and engineering can build without ambiguity.

2. Vision, mission, and promise
2.1 Vision
ASTRIX AI becomes the operating system for product decisions and post-launch accountability in B2B SaaS companies. Every meaningful product change should be traceable to evidence, a decision, and a measurable verdict.

2.2 Mission
Give product teams a single evidence-backed system to decide what to build, ship it, and prove whether it worked.

2.3 Core promise
Know what to build next — and whether it worked — with proof.

2.4 Positioning
Category: Product decision intelligence / product accountability platform
Positioning line: The accountability layer for product decisions
Mental model: The missing layer between scattered feedback and shipped outcomes
Secondary mental model: Cursor for Product Managers, but grounded in structured evidence

3. Problem statement
B2B SaaS product teams receive customer signals from many disconnected sources: support tickets, CS notes, emails, sales calls, spreadsheets, Slack threads, and feature request boards. This creates fragmented evidence, noisy prioritization, and weak post-launch accountability.

Today, teams often suffer from the following:

Feedback is scattered across tools and people.

Loud customers can distort prioritization.

Revenue impact is weakly connected to product prioritization.

Decisions are poorly documented.

Teams launch features but do not consistently measure whether customer pain decreased.

Leadership lacks a clean record of which bets worked and which failed.

ASTRIX AI solves this by creating one controlled system of record:

Signals → Problems → Opportunities → Decisions → Launches → Verdicts

This loop is the product’s defining mechanism and the core scope gate for MVP. Any feature that does not strengthen this loop should be excluded from MVP scope, which aligns with PRD best practice to define scope and out-of-scope items explicitly.

4. Goals
4.1 Business goals
Validate demand with 5–10 design partner B2B SaaS companies.

Convert at least 3 design partners into paying customers within the first structured beta cycle.

Establish ASTRIX AI as a differentiated product around launch accountability, not just feedback capture.

Create a technical and data-model foundation that can later support integrations, enterprise permissions, and deeper analytics.

4.2 Product goals
Reduce time from signup to first ranked opportunity to under 10 minutes.

Make the product usable as a weekly ritual for PMs and Heads of Product.

Ensure every launch in the system can be reviewed at baseline, Day 7, Day 30, and final verdict.

Make AI assistance useful but non-autonomous: draft, explain, summarize, and suggest — never silently decide.

Understand what product problems matter most.

Defend prioritization with evidence.

Record decision rationale clearly.

Track whether launches improved signal volume, affected accounts, and ARR at risk.

6. MVP thesis
ASTRIX AI’s MVP is not a generic feedback board, roadmap tool, or analytics dashboard. It is a product accountability workflow that links evidence, prioritization, decisions, launches, and outcomes in a single operating loop.

6.1 Hero wedge
The strongest initial wedge is post-launch accountability for product teams.

Why this wedge matters:

Feedback aggregation alone is crowded.

Roadmapping alone is crowded.

AI drafting alone is easy to imitate.

Structured verdicts and proof summaries tied to launches are more differentiated and habit-forming.

6.2 Aha moment
A new workspace reaches the first meaningful aha moment when the user has:

Uploaded or loaded at least 20 signals.

Linked signals to at least 5 accounts.

Seen at least 3 generated opportunities with transparent scores.

Opened one opportunity and viewed the evidence behind its ranking.

7. Product principles
Core loop is sacred: Features must strengthen the journey from signal to verdict.

Evidence first: Every meaningful recommendation must connect back to signals, accounts, or metrics.

Account impact over noise: ARR, segment, and affected accounts matter more than raw volume alone.

User in control: AI assists but does not silently change scores, clusters, decisions, or verdicts.

A launch without a verdict is incomplete: The system should nudge until the loop is closed.

Explainability over magic: Scoring and recommendations must be inspectable.

Fast and credible: MVP should feel lightweight and useful within minutes.

9. User journeys
9.1 Primary journey — from signup to verdict
User signs up via email/password or Google.

User creates a workspace and chooses timezone.

User selects either “Load sample workspace” or “Start from scratch.”

User uploads signals CSV or adds signals manually.

User uploads accounts CSV.

System suggests signal-to-account matches by domain or email patterns.

User reviews AI-enriched signal metadata.

User groups signals into problems, manually or using AI suggestions.

System creates and scores opportunities.

User opens a top opportunity and reviews score breakdown and supporting evidence.

User creates a decision: Build, Fix, Experiment, Defer, or Reject.

System drafts a decision memo and optional PRD skeleton.

User creates a launch linked to the decision.

User records baseline values.

System triggers Day 7 reminder.

User completes Day 7 review.

System triggers Day 30 reminder.

User completes Day 30 review.

User submits final verdict.

System generates a proof summary and updates the dashboard.

9.2 Weekly ritual journey
PM opens dashboard on Monday.

Reviews “Top opportunities” and “Reviews due.”

Filters opportunities by segment, product area, or ARR.

Compares 2–3 opportunities.

Creates or updates a decision.

Checks pending launch reviews.

Completes any overdue outcome review.

9.3 Leadership review journey
Founder or HoP opens dashboard or launches list.

Filters launches by verdict, owner, date range, or product area.

Reviews proof summaries for recent launches.

Uses recent decisions and verdict patterns to discuss what worked and what failed.

Fixed MVP scoring model
Total opportunity score = 100 points.

Signal count: 0–30

Affected accounts: 0–20

ARR at risk: 0–30

Severity: 0–10

Recency: 0–10

Scoring definitions
Signal count: normalized based on relative signal count within workspace in the chosen analysis window.

Affected accounts: normalized count of unique linked accounts.

ARR at risk: sum of linked account ARR weighted by churn risk where available.

Severity: derived from weighted mix of linked signal severity values.

Recency: based on freshness of linked signals and recent growth trend.

Formula guidance
For MVP, use deterministic normalization rather than ML scoring. Each factor should calculate a subscore using capped min-max logic within the workspace and current time window, then sum into total score. PRDs should make requirements testable and implementation-friendly, including release criteria and success logic.

12. Non-functional requirements
A complete PRD should include non-functional requirements such as performance, security, and operational constraints in addition to features.

12.1 Performance
App shell should load in under 3 seconds on standard broadband for core authenticated routes.

List pages should return initial results in under 2 seconds for MVP-scale datasets.

CSV import preview should render in under 10 seconds for supported file sizes.

AI generation actions should return draft output or visible progress state within 20 seconds.

12.2 Security
RLS on all workspace-scoped tables.

No client-side secrets.

Sensitive actions only via secure server or edge functions.

Audit logging for critical actions.

HTTPS-only deployment.

Secrets stored in environment configuration.

12.3 Reliability
CSV import failures must be recoverable without corrupting existing data.

Reminder jobs must be idempotent.

AI provider failure must degrade gracefully to manual workflows.

12.4 Accessibility
Keyboard-accessible core flows.

Clear labels, validation, and error states.

Color contrast meeting standard accessibility expectations.

12.5 Explainability
Opportunity score breakdown must always be inspectable.

AI-generated output must be labeled as editable AI output.

Any citation or evidence panel must link to underlying structured records.

13. AI architecture
13.1 Role of AI
AI helps with classification, clustering suggestions, recommendation text, and content drafting. AI does not own final decisions, verdicts, or silent prioritization.

13.2 Providers
Gemini for low-cost and free-tier support use cases.

Groq or equivalent fast providers for premium tiers where latency and quality matter.

Experimental providers may be used behind an internal abstraction layer, but production assumptions must not rely on permanent free credits.

13.3 AI output contract
Every AI response should support the following structure where relevant:

suggested_value

reasoning_summary

evidence_refs

confidence

editable = true

generated_at

provider

13.4 Fallback behavior
If AI fails, allow manual workflow.

If AI confidence is low, present weaker suggestion language.

Never silently commit AI-generated changes.

18. Edge cases and failure states
18.1 CSV edge cases
Missing required columns.

Duplicate identifiers.

Mixed date formats.

Empty rows.

Invalid ARR values.

Oversized files.

Invalid encoding.

18.2 Data quality edge cases
Signals with no account data.

Accounts with multiple domains.

Problems with only one signal.

Opportunities created from sparse or low-confidence data.

Launches missing baseline review.

18.3 AI edge cases
Provider timeout.

Invalid JSON output.

Unsupported classification due to missing raw text.

Hallucinated recommendation not grounded in workspace data.

18.4 UX failure requirements
Every failed operation must return a user-readable message.

Recovery path must be explicit where possible.

User-entered text must not be lost on retryable failures.

19. Risks and mitigations
Risk	Description	Mitigation
Scope creep	Product expands into roadmaps, analytics, or integrations too early	Enforce scope gate tied to the core loop
Over-automation	AI may appear to replace judgment rather than assist	Keep user approval mandatory
Weak RLS	Multi-workspace leakage would be existentially damaging	Test permissions early and repeatedly
CSV complexity	Imports often fail in real-world messy datasets	Build import preview, row-level errors, and retry flows

22. Open questions
These questions should be resolved before final engineering lock:

What exact formula will convert ARR, churn risk, and recency into normalized opportunity subscores?

Will baseline metrics be always auto-derived, always manually editable, or hybrid by field?

What is the exact maximum CSV size and row count for MVP?

Should members be able to delete decisions, launches, or verdicts, or only archive them?

How will AI evidence references be rendered in UI — inline citations, side panel links, or both?

Is compare mode MVP-critical or launchable shortly after MVP?

Does billing enforcement happen synchronously in app actions, via webhook-updated entitlements, or both?
